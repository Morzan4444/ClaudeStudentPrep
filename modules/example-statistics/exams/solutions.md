**Q1: B) 95%**
The empirical rule states that 95% of values in a normal distribution fall within 2 standard deviations of the mean. Here, 30 = 50 − 2×10 and 70 = 50 + 2×10, which is exactly the ±2σ range.

## Short Answer Solutions

**Q6:**
Population variance (σ²) is calculated by dividing the sum of squared deviations by N (the entire population size). Sample variance (s²) divides by n−1 instead of n. This correction — called Bessel's correction — is necessary because a sample tends to underestimate the true spread of the population: the sample data points cluster closer to the sample mean than to the true population mean. Dividing by n−1 inflates the estimate slightly, making it an unbiased estimator of σ². As n grows large, the difference between dividing by n and n−1 becomes negligible.

**Q7:**
The p-value does NOT prove that H₀ is false. The p-value (0.001) is the probability of observing a result as extreme as the data — or more extreme — *assuming H₀ is true*. A very small p-value means the data would be very unlikely if H₀ were true, which gives us strong reason to reject H₀. However, this is a probabilistic statement, not proof. H₀ could still be true and we are observing a rare event. The correct statement is: "We reject H₀ at the 0.1% significance level; the data provide very strong evidence against the null hypothesis."

**Q8:**
A 95% confidence interval means: if we repeated this study many times and built a CI each time, approximately 95% of those intervals would contain the true population mean. It does NOT mean there is a 95% probability that the true mean lies in [42, 58] — the true mean either is or is not in that specific interval. From CI = [42, 58] we can conclude: our best estimate of the true mean test score is 50 (the midpoint), and the data are consistent with the true mean being anywhere from 42 to 58. We cannot conclude that individual students scored between 42 and 58 — this is about the mean, not individual scores.

