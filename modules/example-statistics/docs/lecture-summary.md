## Overview

This lecture introduces the foundational concepts of descriptive and inferential statistics. Students learn how to summarize datasets using measures of central tendency and variability, understand probability distributions, and apply hypothesis testing to draw conclusions from sample data. These tools form the backbone of quantitative reasoning in science, business, and engineering.

## Main Themes

1. **Measures of Central Tendency** — Mean, median, and mode as ways to describe the "center" of a dataset
2. **Measures of Dispersion** — Variance, standard deviation, and range describing how spread out data is
3. **Probability Distributions** — Normal distribution, its properties, and the empirical rule (68-95-99.7)
4. **The Central Limit Theorem** — Why sample means tend toward a normal distribution regardless of population shape
5. **Hypothesis Testing** — Null and alternative hypotheses, p-values, and significance levels
6. **Confidence Intervals** — Estimating population parameters from sample statistics
7. **Correlation and Regression** — Measuring linear relationships between two variables

## Conclusion

Statistics provides the mathematical language for reasoning under uncertainty. Mastery of these concepts is essential for interpreting research findings, building predictive models, and making data-driven decisions. Every subsequent topic in quantitative courses builds on these foundations.
